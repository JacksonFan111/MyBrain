Below is a detailed evaluation of how a Power BI engine would process each DAX measure in the script—keeping in mind that the 'Account Holdings Monthly' table contains only month‑end snapshot data. This means that all calculations using that table will reflect values as of the last day of the month. I've grouped the measures by their purpose and added commentary on both the filter context and any implications from using month‑end data.

1. Term Deposit Measures (Using 'Account Holdings Monthly')

  • TotalTDIncAI

    • What It Does:
      Sums the total term deposit income (in NZD) from the month‑end snapshot.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filters the table by:

        • The valuation method ("Accumulation"),

        • A specific reporting date (which is a month‑end date, injected via the parameter),

        • Asset type equal to "Term Deposit",

        • Ensuring the FixedInterestAssetKey is valid (not blank or -1).

      • Sums the column [Holding NZD Total].

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Since the table holds month‑end data, the engine only calculates the snapshot values at that period. No intra‑month variations are captured.

  • TotalTDAI

    • What It Does:
      Sums accrued interest (in NZD) for term deposits using the same filters.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Applies identical filtering (Accumulation, term deposit, reporting date, valid key).

      • Sums [NZD Accrued Interest].

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Again, this measure reflects only the snapshot at month‑end.

  • TotalTDIncAILocal & TotalTDAILocal

    • What They Do:
      They compute term deposit income and accrued interest in the local currency.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Use the same filter context as above, but work on local total columns.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      The same month‑end snapshot logic applies.

  • TotalTDFaceValueLocal & TotalTDFaceValue

    • What They Do:
      They subtract accrued interest from income (in local currency and NZD, respectively) to determine the net face value.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • These measures rely on the previously computed totals.

      • The engine simply performs an arithmetic subtraction in the current filter context (the month‑end snapshot).

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Calculations use month‑end values, so trends within the month aren’t visible.

2. Transaction Measures (Using 'Asset Transaction')

  • TotalTDInterestPaid & TotalTDTaxPaid

    • What They Do:
      They capture interest paid and tax paid on term deposit transactions, respectively.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filter the Asset Transaction table for transactions of type "Income" or "Coupon" where the asset type is "Term Deposit."

      • Further restrict using the Traded Date table to include only rows belonging to the given financial year.

      • Sum the respective columns (Amount for interest paid and Tax Amount NZD for tax paid).

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Although these transactions come from a transactional table (likely capturing daily activity), when combined with month‑end snapshots in reporting, they may be aggregated to compare with the month‑end balance.

  • TotalTDInterestGross

    • What It Does:
      Adds interest paid and tax paid to produce a gross interest value.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • The engine performs a straightforward addition of the two measures above.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      The gross figure represents the month‑end accumulation of these transactional items.

  • TotalCMInterest, TotalCMTax, & TotalCMGross

    • What They Do:
      These measures work similarly to the term deposit transaction measures but apply to cash management transactions.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filters on transactions flagged as "Cashman" (yes) and then applies the appropriate transaction type filter.

      • Uses the financial year from the Traded Date.

      • Sums up the corresponding columns.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      When these transactional amounts are compared with month‑end snapshots (such as balances), they serve to explain changes from day‑to‑day within the period ending at month‑end.

  • TotalCMBalance

    • What It Does:
      Calculates the cash management holding balance from the month‑end snapshot.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Uses the same filters as the term deposit measures but for Cashman assets.

      • Sums the Holding NZD Total column for those rows.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      This measure is a snapshot value representing the balance at the month‑end date.

3. CSL Measures (For Non-Cashman, Non-Term Deposit Assets)

  • TotalCSLIntTax, TotalCSLIntNet, & TotalCSLIntGross

    • What They Do:
      They calculate the interest tax, net interest, and gross interest for non-Cashman transactions (and assets not classified as term deposits) using data provider 20.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • The engine applies filters to exclude Cashman and term deposit assets.

      • For TotalCSLIntTax, it picks the Tax Amount NZD.

      • For TotalCSLIntNet, it sums the Amount.

      • TotalCSLIntGross simply adds these two.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Although these measures are defined on the transactional table, when viewed in reports alongside month‑end snapshots, they provide period totals rather than daily granularity.

  • TotalCSLDivTax, TotalCSLDivNet, & TotalCSLDivGross

    • What They Do:
      These measures calculate the dividend tax, net dividend, and gross dividend for non-Cashman transactions (again excluding term deposits) by applying filters that remove "Income" or "Coupon" transactions.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Similar to the interest measures, but the filter logic focuses on dividend (non-income) transactions.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      These provide period totals that, when combined with month‑end balances, help explain dividend distributions.

4. MPS and START Scheme Measures

  • TotalHoldingMPS

    • What It Does:
      Calculates holdings for the MPS category from the month‑end snapshot.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filters the Account Holdings Monthly table for assets meeting specific criteria:

        • Valuation Method is "Accumulation."

        • The asset’s sedol is not "Unsupervised."

        • The Service Levels display name contains one of the specified codes (MPS, IAS, DIMS) via PATHCONTAINS.

        • Market Code is not "Unlisted."

      • Sums Holding NZD Total for these rows.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      This measure uses the month‑end snapshot to deliver a single aggregated value for MPS-related holdings.

  • TotalSTART

    • What It Does:
      Sums the holdings for the START scheme (managed by NZXWT).

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filters the Account Holdings Monthly table where the service level type equals "START" and sums the Holding NZD Total.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      Since this is sourced from month‑end data, it only captures the START scheme value as of the month‑end.

  • TotalHoldingSC

    • What It Does:
      Calculates the safe-keeping balance for holdings.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Filters out rows with unsupervised sedols and unlisted market codes.

      • Sums the Holding NZD SafeKeeping column.

    • Month‑End Implication:
      As with other month‑end measures, it captures the snapshot value for safe-keeping balances.

  • TotalHolding

    • What It Does:
      Provides the overall total holding by combining the safe-keeping balance, MPS holdings, and START scheme holdings.

    • Evaluation Process:

      • Sums the three component measures.

      • Applies a final filter ensuring the holdings date matches the reporting date (which is a month‑end date).

    • Month‑End Implication:
      This measure is a composite snapshot that only reflects the state at month‑end.

Because 'Account Holdings Monthly' only contains month‑end snapshots, a Power BI expert would note:

  • Snapshot Nature:
    All measures drawing from this table will reflect a single point in time (the last day of the month). If you need intra‑month detail, additional tables or data sources would be required.

  • Time Intelligence Considerations:
    When using these measures in Power BI, you must be aware that trends over time will only show month‑end values. Slicers or visuals comparing daily data might not work as expected if the underlying data is only monthly.

  • Aggregation Context:
    The engine will evaluate these measures based solely on the snapshot. For example, TotalTDIncAI will always pull the value as of the month‑end and will not change during the month.

  • Modified Approach:
    If intra‑month trends or more granular analysis is needed, consider integrating a daily snapshot table or adjusting the data refresh so that more frequent snapshots are captured. Otherwise, the model as-is is ideal for month‑end financial reporting.

Below is an explanation of the financial meaning behind these measures—what they represent in our model and why they’re important:

1. Term Deposit Measures

  • TotalTDIncAI
    What It Means:
    This measure calculates the total income (the overall value) of term deposits at month‑end in NZD.
    Why It Matters:
    It tells you the full amount held in term deposits as of the snapshot date—an important figure for assessing overall investment size.

  • TotalTDAI
    What It Means:
    This measure sums up the accrued interest on term deposits. “Accrued” means the interest has been earned over time, even if it hasn’t yet been paid out.
    Why It Matters:
    Accrued interest reflects earnings that have built up. It shows how much additional value has been earned on top of the deposited principal, which is crucial for accurate financial reporting.

  • TotalTDIncAILocal & TotalTDAILocal
    What They Mean:
    These are the same as the previous two measures but expressed in the local currency instead of NZD.
    Why They Matter:
    They allow comparison or consolidation when the bank operates in multiple currencies, ensuring all values are accurately captured in the appropriate currency context.

  • TotalTDFaceValueLocal & TotalTDFaceValue
    What They Mean:
    These are calculated by subtracting the accrued interest from the total income. They represent the “net” deposit amount (the original principal) in both local currency and NZD.
    Why They Matter:
    Understanding the net value is key to determining the true underlying deposit after accounting for interest earnings.

2. Transaction Measures for Term Deposits

  • TotalTDInterestPaid
    What It Means:
    This measure sums up the actual interest payments made on term deposits—capturing the cash that has been paid out as interest.
    Why It Matters:
    It shows the real cash flow from interest payments, which is useful for reconciling with bank statements.

  • TotalTDTaxPaid
    What It Means:
    It captures the tax amounts withheld or paid on those term deposit interest payments.
    Why It Matters:
    Tax impacts the net income received. This measure is essential for understanding the full cost/benefit of term deposit earnings.

  • TotalTDInterestGross
    What It Means:
    This is the sum of interest paid and the tax paid, giving the “gross” interest figure before tax is deducted.
    Why It Matters:
    It provides a complete view of the earnings potential of the term deposits without the effect of tax deductions, useful for performance comparisons.

3. Cash Management Measures

  • TotalCMInterest, TotalCMTax, & TotalCMGross
    What They Mean:
    These measures capture the cash management side:

    • TotalCMInterest: Interest earned on daily cash management transactions.

    • TotalCMTax: Tax applied on these cash management transactions.

    • TotalCMGross: Their sum, representing the gross cash management earnings. Why They Matter:
      They help assess the performance and tax impact of short‑term liquidity management—critical for managing everyday cash flows.

  • TotalCMBalance
    What It Means:
    This measure gives the balance of the cash management account as seen in the month‑end snapshot.
    Why It Matters:
    It indicates how much cash is available, which is key for day‑to‑day operations and liquidity analysis.

4. CSL (Special Cash Holdings) Measures

  • TotalCSLIntTax, TotalCSLIntNet, & TotalCSLIntGross
    What They Mean:

    • TotalCSLIntTax: Tax on interest for a special group of transactions (non-Cashman and not term deposits).

    • TotalCSLIntNet: The net interest earned in these transactions.

    • TotalCSLIntGross: The sum of net interest and its tax, providing the gross amount. Why They Matter:
      They offer insight into a specific set of cash holdings managed under special arrangements, helping auditors and managers understand the performance of these niche investments.

  • TotalCSLDivTax, TotalCSLDivNet, & TotalCSLDivGross
    What They Mean:
    These work like the interest measures but focus on dividend payments (money distributed from profits).
    Why They Matter:
    They help clarify how much dividend income is generated and how much tax is applied, which is essential for net income calculations.

5. MPS Holdings and START Scheme Measures

  • TotalHoldingMPS
    What It Means:
    This measure sums up the holdings for investments identified by specific codes (like MPS, IAS, or DIMS).
    Why It Matters:
    It provides a total value for these particular types of investments, offering a focused view on a segment of the portfolio.

  • TotalSTART, TotalHoldingSC, & TotalHolding
    What They Mean:

    • TotalSTART: Total value of the START scheme holdings (a separate investment managed by another company).

    • TotalHoldingSC: The safe-keeping balance, which is money kept in a secure holding account.

    • TotalHolding: The overall total, combining safe-keeping, MPS holdings, and START scheme holdings. Why They Matter:
      These measures give a complete picture of the portfolio’s value by aggregating all different types of holdings. This overall total is critical for assessing the institution’s financial health and for making strategic decisions.

Bringing It All Together

  • Dynamic Parameters:
    All these measures are built to use a specific reporting date (always a month‑end) and financial year. This consistency ensures that every calculation refers to the same point in time—making the report accurate and reliable.

  • Filter Context:
    Each measure applies filters to focus only on the relevant data (like term deposits, Cashman, or non-Cashman assets). This is like using a sieve to pick out only the pieces you need for each calculation, ensuring accuracy.

  • Aggregation and Netting:
    Many measures work by summing up amounts and then subtracting related values (such as subtracting accrued interest from total income to find the net face value). These arithmetic operations provide the “net” or “gross” figures necessary for understanding overall performance.
