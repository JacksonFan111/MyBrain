Below is a summarized overview of the discussions and logic clarifications around the 16.12.2024 FATCA/CRS bug fix, suitable for a Confluence knowledge base entry.

Date: 16 December 2024
Topic: FATCA/CRS Reporting Bug Fix & Data Quality Issues

Key Issues Identified:

  1. Individual Accounts with Multiple Persons/Controlling Roles

    • Certain individual accounts are found to have multiple associated individuals (e.g., authorized persons, controlling persons) who may be US or CRS reportable, even when the account holder is not a US person.

    • Example:
      Mrs. Zeta Eleanor Robertson (Individual) has multiple controlling persons, including one with US indicia. This scenario complicates FATCA reporting as it appears to be an “Individual” account but behaves more like a structure with multiple controlling individuals.

  2. Data Quality vs. System Logic

    • Discrepancies arise due to data classification issues in CRM (e.g., accounts originally joint but converted to individual after one party’s death, yet roles and tax indicators remain inconsistent).

    • Example:
      Frances Anne Stone (602680) was previously in a joint account that became an individual account after the co-owner’s death. However, an authorized person with US/UK tax indicia remains on the account, causing confusion in the reporting logic.

  3. FATCA/CRS Reporting Logic Challenges

    • For an individual account, standard logic assumes a single account holder with no controlling persons. Yet, exceptions are found where authorized or controlling persons have foreign tax indicia.

    • If a US (or CRS-reportable) person has control or authority over the account, this triggers reporting obligations, even if the primary account holder is not US/CRS-reportable.

    • The system must handle cases where:

      • Non-US individual accounts have US controlling persons (FATCA).

      • Non-CRS individual accounts have CRS-reportable controlling persons (CRS).

  4. FATCA Reporting Specifics

    • If the primary individual account holder is not a US person and has no US TIN, use the code "999999999" as per guidance.

    • The presence of a US controlling person requires reporting the account under FATCA category:
      1 - Owner-Documented FFI with Substantial U.S. Owners / Controlling Persons (FATCA101).

    • Even though the account is “individual,” if there is a substantial US controlling person, that person must appear on the FATCA report.

  5. CRS Reporting Specifics

    • Similar logic applies to CRS. If an authorized person or controlling person has CRS indicia (e.g., UK resident with a NIN), the account becomes CRS-reportable.

    • Poor data quality (incorrect entity types, roles, or missing foreign tax flags) can cause under- or over-reporting.

Resolutions and Recommendations:

  • Data Quality Improvements:

    • Ensure CRM data is accurate and consistent:

      • Correctly classify the entity type (Individual vs. Joint vs. Trust).

      • Update roles (e.g., remove “Other” role if now “Authorised Person”).

      • Maintain correct ownership percentages and foreign tax flags.

    • Data corrections must be completed before the reporting deadlines to avoid incorrect FATCA/CRS submissions.

  • System Logic Adjustments:

    • Update logic to handle scenarios where individual accounts have controlling persons with reportable tax indicia.

    • When encountering unusual data scenarios (e.g., multiple controlling persons for an individual account), treat them as reportable if any controlling person meets US/CRS criteria.

    • Avoid creating exceptions or “hard-coding” around poor data. Instead, ensure the data is rectified so that the logic can remain straightforward and rule-based.

  • Validation and Exception Reporting:

    • Implement exception reports to flag questionable data (e.g., individual accounts with multiple controllers, missing TINs, or incorrect entity classifications).

    • Provide these reports to data stewards to correct records before final FATCA/CRS runs.

Key Takeaways:

  • Individual accounts can still trigger FATCA/CRS reporting if non-account-holder individuals (controlling, authorized) have US or CRS indicia.

  • The code should not attempt to solve data quality issues. Instead, CRM data must be corrected to reflect true account structures and tax statuses.

  • Accurate, black-and-white logic is only possible when underlying data is consistent and correct.

  • The “999999999” code should be used for FATCA TIN fields for non-US account holders without a US TIN.

This summary highlights the importance of data integrity and the need for clear, consistent rules to ensure correct and compliant FATCA/CRS reporting.
